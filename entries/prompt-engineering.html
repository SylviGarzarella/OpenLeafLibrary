Prompt Engineering —
How to Actually Talk to AI

Most people treat AI like a search engine. Type something vague, hope for the best. But the way you phrase your prompt is everything — and once you understand that, the whole thing changes.

AI Prompt Engineering ChatGPT LLM Productivity

Let's be honest — most of us started out typing one-liners into ChatGPT and wondering why the answers felt so generic. The tool is powerful, but only as powerful as the instructions you give it. Think of it like briefing a very capable colleague who knows absolutely nothing about your specific situation. The more context you provide, the better the result. Every single time.

The quality of your output is a direct reflection of the quality of your input.

How Does an LLM Actually Work?

Before we get into prompting, it helps to understand what's happening under the hood. A Large Language Model predicts, token by token, what word is most likely to come next based on everything it learned during training.

A token is the smallest unit of text a model processes. Each token is converted into a vector and connected to others through Attention — how the model understands relationships between concepts. Here's what tokenisation looks like in practice:

I have hunger . Every word and punctuation mark is one token .

One parameter worth knowing is Temperature — it controls how creative or predictable the model's responses are:

Low Temperature
Consistent & predictable
Focused and careful
Best for: facts, logic, summaries
High Temperature
Creative & playful
Surprising, sometimes unpredictable
Best for: brainstorming, creative writing

Also keep in mind: every model has a token limit. Both input and output count toward it. Once exceeded, the model starts losing earlier parts of your conversation — so for long sessions, open a fresh chat rather than cramming everything into one.

The Anatomy of a Good Prompt

A good prompt isn't just a question — it's a brief. There are six core elements that make the difference between a vague request and a result you can actually use:

01
Role
Who should the AI be? A tutor, a critic, a domain expert?
02
Context
Your background, your goal, where you're at right now.
03
Instruction
The actual task. What exactly do you want done?
04
Format
Table, bullet points, step-by-step — how should the answer look?
05
Constraints
What to leave out. Stay on topic, keep it simple, no jargon.
06
Example
Optional but powerful. Show the model what a good response looks like.

Bad Prompt vs. Good Prompt — The Difference

Same topic, completely different results. The easiest way to understand prompting is to see it side by side.

Scenario 1 — Studying & Learning

ChatGPT ✗ Weak prompt
Explain reaction mechanisms.
You
AI
A reaction mechanism describes the step-by-step process by which a chemical reaction occurs, including bond-breaking and bond-forming steps, intermediates, and transition states. Common types include SN1, SN2, E1, and E2 reactions...
ChatGPT ✓ Strong prompt
You are my tutor for Organic Chemistry. I'm a 2nd semester Food Technology student struggling to see the bigger picture. Walk me through reaction mechanisms step by step — explain like I'm in 5th grade, use vivid examples, and end with a short summary table.
You
AI
Great — let's build this from the ground up! Think of a reaction mechanism like a recipe: it tells us exactly what happens, step by step, when atoms get rearranged. First, let's talk about why atoms even want to react with each other...

Why it works: Role, academic context, specific topic, preferred explanation style, and output format are all defined. The model knows exactly who it's talking to — and responds accordingly.

Scenario 2 — Writing a Text

✗ Weak
"Write a follow-up email for a job application."
✓ Strong
"Write a professional follow-up email for an application I sent two weeks ago to a marketing agency. Confident but not pushy, max 5 sentences, end with a clear call to action."

Why it works: Industry, timing, tone, length and structure are all defined. The model doesn't have to guess — it just delivers.

Scenario 3 — Planning & Organisation

✗ Weak
"Help me plan my week."
✓ Strong
"I have [task list]. I work best in the mornings, lectures on Tuesday & Thursday 10–2pm. Create a daily plan with breaks that finishes everything by Friday evening."

Why it works: Real constraints, real preferences, a clear output format. The more specific the input, the more useful the result.

Scenario 4 — Creative Work

✗ Weak
"Write something about electric cars."
✓ Strong
"Write a discussion about electric cars from three perspectives: an engineer, a mechanic, and a car salesperson. One paragraph each. Highlight where their opinions conflict."

Why it works: Multiple perspectives, clear structure, specific focus. The result is actually interesting to read — not a generic overview.

Four Techniques Worth Knowing

Beyond the basics, these are the prompting techniques that consistently shift the quality of what you get back.

01 Prompt Priming

Give the model context before you ask your actual question. The more it knows about you upfront — background, level, goals — the better every answer will be throughout the entire conversation.

"Before we start — I'm a student with a limited budget who wants to eat healthier. I have no idea what gut-friendly nutrition even means yet. With that in mind: what are gut-friendly foods and where do I begin?"
02 Chain of Thought

Ask the model to think step by step. Essential for complex topics or anything where logical reasoning matters. It prevents the model from jumping straight to a conclusion and missing key steps.

"Explain the concept of supply and demand step by step, starting from the very basics. Don't skip any steps and check in with me before moving to the next one."
03 Perspective Shift

Ask for the same topic from different angles. Great for fully understanding a subject or thinking critically. You can request a debate, a comparison, or simply multiple viewpoints on the same issue.

"Write about remote work from the perspective of a CEO, an employee, and a psychologist. One paragraph each — highlight where they agree and where they conflict."
04 Self-Critique Loop

Ask the model to generate something — then immediately critique it. The model becomes its own editor. Works surprisingly well for writing, structuring arguments, or checking whether a summary actually holds up.

"Write a summary of [topic] and then critique it on its structure, relevance, and clarity. Give me concrete suggestions on how to improve it."

The one thing to remember: AI doesn't read minds — and it shouldn't have to. The more context, structure and specificity you bring to a prompt, the more precise the response. A vague question gets a vague answer. A well-structured brief gets a result you can actually use.

Prompting is a skill — and like any skill, it gets better with practice. Save the prompts that worked, iterate on the ones that didn't, and don't be frustrated if the first attempt isn't perfect. You can even ask the model itself what's missing from your prompt and how to make it more specific. It'll tell you.
